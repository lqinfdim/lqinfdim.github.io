Abstract

The reasoning large language model (RLLM) has been proven competitive in solving complex reasoning tasks such as mathematics, coding, compared to general LLM. However, the serving performance and behavior of RLLM remains unexplored, which may undermine the deployment and utilization of RLLM in real-world scenario. To close this gap, in this paper, we conduct a comprehensive study of RLLM service. We first perform a pilot study on comparing the serving performance between RLLM and traditional LLM and reveal that there are several distinct differences regarding serving behavior: (1) significant memory usage and fluctuations; (2) straggler requests; (3) adaptive running time; (4) domain preference. Then we further investigate whether existing inference optimization techniques are valid for RLLM. Our main takeaways are that model quantization methods and speculative decoding can improve service system efficiency with a small compromise to RLLM accuracy, while prefix caching, KV cache quantization may even degrade accuracy or serving performance for small RLLM. Lastly, we conductevaluation under real world workload modeled by Gamma distribution to verify our findings. Empirical results for real world workload evaluation across different dataset are aligned with our main findings regarding RLLM serving. We hope our work can provide the research community and industry with insights to advance RLLM inference serving.

Assessment Framework & Experimental Setup

The paper studies whether reasoning LLMs introduce serving behaviors that existing LLM inference engines were not designed for. To answer this question, the authors define the ASU framework and benchmark matched LLM/RLLM pairs with ASU-Perf across multiple model scales, datasets, and open-source serving engines.

ASU

Accuracy

Each optimization is evaluated against benchmark accuracy, so serving gains are always interpreted together with answer quality.

ASU

Service-end

The provider view focuses on throughput, especially tokens per second (TPS), to reflect system efficiency under load.

ASU

User-end

The user view tracks time to first visible token (TTFVT) and end-to-end running time, emphasizing perceived responsiveness.

Models

Matched LLM / RLLM pairs

The study compares Qwen-2.5-Math and Llama models with DeepSeek-R1-Distill counterparts at 7B, 14B, 32B, and 70B scales.

Datasets

Math-heavy plus knowledge reasoning

GSM8K, MATH-500, and AIME-2024 cover easy-to-hard math reasoning, while GPQA represents knowledge reasoning.

Engines

vLLM and SGLang

Both experiments and workload studies are carried out on two widely adopted open-source inference engines via OpenAI-compatible APIs.

Benchmark

ASU-Perf

The benchmark suite is used throughout the paper to compare serving traces, optimization results, and real-world workload behavior.

Pilot Study: Serving LLM vs. RLLM

The pilot study compares matched 7B LLM and RLLM models under batch serving. Its central visualization reveals why reasoning workloads stress inference systems differently from conventional LLM requests.

Suggested image: paper Figure 2. This figure compares KV-cache usage, number of running requests, running time, and task performance for matched 7B LLM/RLLM pairs across GSM8K, MATH-500, AIME-2024, and GPQA.

Finding 1

Significant Memory Usage and Fluctuations

RLLMs place much heavier pressure on KV cache than standard LLMs, and cache usage rises and falls sharply as long reasoning traces are created and then discarded.

Finding 2

Straggler Requests

Hard requests linger far longer than easy ones, stretching batch completion time and leaving the engine with a long latency tail.

Finding 3

Adaptive Running Time

For RLLMs, runtime varies strongly with task difficulty because harder problems trigger longer reasoning chains during decoding.

Finding 4

Domain Preference on Math

The performance gap between RLLMs and LLMs is largest on mathematical reasoning tasks, while the two are much closer on GPQA-style knowledge reasoning.

Optimization Study: Existing Techniques under RLLM Serving

Section 5 examines whether familiar LLM serving optimizations remain effective once requests become decode-heavy, memory-intensive, and highly variable in length.

Model Weight Quantization

KV Cache Quantization

Suggested image: paper Figure 4. For larger RLLMs, KV-cache quantization improves runtime efficiency and often helps TTFVT and TPS, but the paper notes that smaller 7B models can suffer severe degradation.

Prefix Caching

Suggested image: paper Figure 5. Prefix caching benefits sufficiently large models, especially 14B and above, but can introduce extra latency for smaller models when prefix reuse is limited.

The paper then replaces synchronized batches with bursty workloads modeled by a Gamma distribution, verifying whether the pilot-study behaviors still appear when requests arrive continuously.

Implications for Future RLLM Systems

Direction 1

Finer-grained memory management

Long and volatile reasoning traces call for better cache lifetime control, re-prefill / reload policies, and more adaptive KV management during decoding.

Direction 2

Difficulty-aware scheduling

Because hard queries dominate tail latency, future systems should prioritize, reorder, or schedule requests dynamically according to estimated reasoning difficulty.

Direction 3

Asymmetric prefill / decode allocation

RLLMs are more decode-heavy than standard LLMs, so resource allocation should emphasize decode workers rather than treating prefill and decode symmetrically.

Direction 4

Hardware-software co-design

Longer generations increase bandwidth demand and KV-access intensity, motivating accelerators and runtimes that are explicitly tuned for RLLM decoding behavior.
